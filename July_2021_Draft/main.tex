\begin{abstract}
\noindent Certificate-of-need laws in 35 US states require nursing homes to show that their region needs the service before offering any additional beds. This paper analyzes the effect of these supply constraints on Medicaid and total nursing home expenditure, as well as the quantity of nursing homes and nursing home beds. We develop a model where firms compete for a mobile nursing home consumer and show that repealing this regulation leads to an increase in the quantity of nursing home services, Medicaid expenditures, and total expenditures. Applying a synthetic difference in differences analysis of the repeal of nursing home certificate-of-need laws, we find that it led to an increase in Medicaid nursing home expenditures of 68 percent and 39 percent in Pennsylvania and Indiana respectively, while the effect in North Dakota is not reliable due to a lack of pre-treatment parallel trends. We also find that this repeal caused an increase in the quantity of nursing homes, nursing home beds, and total nursing home expenditure in all three states. All results where we find evidence for parallel trends are consistent with the predictions of our model.
